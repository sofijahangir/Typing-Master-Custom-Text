Overheard in Curfew
Amidst a communication clampdown, in Kashmir the only news we rely on right now is snatches of information – local happenings transmitted in person in the absence of other modes. People going from door to door, neighbours meeting in the few hours in the morning, on their way to buy bread. As someone who works on the ethnography of communication, an analysis of the wider socio-political context of speech in Kashmir has been fascinating. While investigating how Kashmiris articulate their imagination of a nation through speech, I discovered that the imagination is accompanied by other performative acts that reinforce not just a distinct national identity but also an ethnic one. For long, hearing a Kashmiri speak should have been enough for the Indian state to understand how they’re viewed. James Scott says that one need not wait ‘for open social protest to lift a view of consent and quiescence’ (Scott, 1990; p20). It is reminiscent of a former Kashmiri Chief Minister publicly claiming that anti-national slogans were normal in Kashmir Verbal articulations and racial epithets give us a critical vantage point in understanding the psyche of a people. It is known that India is not only treated as an oppressor in speech but also Indians are seen as ethnic others – foreigners to Kashmir. The need of the hour is to document how everyday lives are being lived in this blackout and what the voices that are left unheard say – not the voices of the opinion-makers, reporters or journalists but the quotidian voices of how life is lived in the circumstances of today. I spent ten days in Srinagar – sans any mode of communication and any affiliations or contact with local news networks. Below is my compiled list of all interactions with people, verbatim, who I came across or heard from. They sketch a picture of what the city of Srinagar looks and feels like right now. It may do well to remind ourselves at this point that we live in the year 2019.
